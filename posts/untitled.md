All Models Are Wrong, Most Are Useful:  Human-AI Collaboration in Cybersecurity Workflows 

Abstract 

This study investigates how cybersecurity professionals integrate Foundation AI (FAI) systems into their workflows, examining perceptions, new behaviors, and the dynamics of human control. Drawing on expert interviews with 20 cybersecurity professionals, the research identifies key thematic factors that shape human–FAI configurations at work, including professional experience and reliability concerns. Findings reveal that FAI adoption entails shared control arrangements wherein decision-making power is continuously exchanged in a human-FAI entanglement, but humans retain final authority over critical decisions. However, adoption frequently outpaces the development of systematic risk mapping or control processes, creating operational tensions wherein practitioners must navigate FAI integration through trial-and-error experimentation and resulting in highly idiosyncratic workflows that vary considerably across individuals and teams. Given the limited accessibility of expert cybersecurity practitioners, this study provides an empirical contribution to understanding FAI adoption dynamics, addressing a research gap at the intersection of FAI adoption and cybersecurity practice. 

**Keywords:** Artificial Intelligence, LLMs, cybersecurity, workflows, human–AI collaboration, human control, agents
